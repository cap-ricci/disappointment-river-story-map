>How it works

An introduction to using the Disappoinment River webmap

The story of the Northwest Passage in a map

In this webmap, the story of the Northwest Passage can be experienced from a variety of perspectives. Explore the Canadian Arctic while following the various trips of the first Europeans to push for a passage between the Atlantic and Pacific Ocean in the north of Canada. Learn about their stops, adventures and discoveries while following a specific journey or look at the timeline of all travels to get an overview of the hardships involved in finding the Northwest Passage. You can add further information to reflect on the changes in the region due to climate change and natural resources. If you ever want to know more about a topic, follow the links provided for almost any piece of information. In the following, there will be a detailed description of all the features you can add to the map. Enjoy combining the different features of the map!

Base Map

Here you can change between three different base maps which constitute the background of the web map. The page will open set on the Stamen Watercolor, but it can be changed at any point in the exploration of the map. The BING Aerial with labels offers a satellite overview of the world, while Stamen Terrain has a focus on topography.

Layers
The next section shows different layers which can be added to the map. By clicking the checkbox on the left side of the layer name, the layer is added. By unchecking the box, the layer is removed. A newly added layer will always be on top of all the previously loaded ones. The position of the layers can be changed by clicking on the arrows on the right side of the layer name. The first arrow moves the layer one up, the second one moves it one down.

  • The Information points show different pieces of information about the Canadian Arctic which are loosely connected to the different explorers or just interesting stories which could not be included in the timelines. Click on the points to show the information panel.
  • The Travel lines add the lines corresponding to the voyages of explorers presented in the map.
  • The Native lands provides some information about the native population of North America and shows their territories.
  • The Canadian relief layer gives more information on the topography of present-day Canada.

Controllers
The controllers are the central piece of the webmap. The default setting is None, which means that no controller is currently selected. You can then choose from a selection of three different options.

  • Choosing the Storymap option opens a new menu on the left side of the page. You can then choose one story to follow. Like this, you can chronologically follow the discoveries of James Cook, Samuel Hearne, Peter Pond, Alexander Mackenzie, Brian Castner and Alistair Mackay. By clicking on the story you want to investigate, a new control panel will open on the left side of the map. You can then scroll through the different steps of the panel, which will simultaneously jump to the corresponding geographic location on the map.
  • The Ice-timeline shows some of the current effects of climate change on the Arctic. By clicking on the arrow on the left side of the timeline, the extension of the Arctic ice sheet (measured at its maximum in September) is animated from 1980 to the present day. You will see how the area of Arctic ice has been shrinking with time.
  • The Events-timeline: 18th Cent. shows the points of interest of the 18th century exploration stories on one shared timeline. You can navigate the information points by either clicking on the event in the timeline, which will zoom the corresponding geographic location and display the information panel. Or you can go the other way around, and freely click on information points to show the event in the timeline and the panel.
  • The Events-timeline: Modern shows the points of interest of the current exploration stories on one shared timeline. As with the 18th century version, you can navigate around the timeline and navigation points as you wish.
